# Euclidean Rhythms 101

Euclidean rhythms distribute a number of beats as evenly as possible across a number of steps. They appear in music traditions worldwide—from West African bell patterns to Cuban clave to electronic music.

In Strudel, you create them with the `euclid` function.

## Your First Euclidean Rhythm

Let's put 3 kicks across 8 steps:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid(3, 8)`} punchcard />

Hit play. You'll hear the beats distributed evenly: **X** . . **X** . . **X** .

The algorithm spreads 3 beats across 8 slots as evenly as it can.

## Try Different Numbers

What happens when you change the values?

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid(5, 8)`} punchcard />

Now you have 5 beats in 8 steps. More beats = denser rhythm.

**Try it:** Change `5` to `2`. Then try `7`. Listen to how the feel changes.

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid(2, 8)`} punchcard />

## Classic Patterns

Many traditional rhythms are euclidean. Here's the Cuban tresillo (3 over 8):

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid(3, 8)`} punchcard />

The West African bell pattern (7 over 12):

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("metal").euclid(7, 12)`} punchcard />

The clave (5 over 16):

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("wood").euclid(5, 16)`} punchcard />

## Adding Rotation

The third parameter rotates the pattern. This shifts where it starts:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid(3, 8, 0)`} punchcard />

Now with rotation of 2:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid(3, 8, 2)`} punchcard />

Same beats, different starting point. Rotation changes the feel without changing the density.

**Try it:** Change the rotation from `0` to `4`. Notice how the downbeat shifts.

## Layering Euclidean Rhythms

The magic happens when you combine multiple euclidean patterns:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`stack(
  sound("bd").euclid(3, 8),
  sound("sd").euclid(2, 8, 1),
  sound("hh").euclid(5, 8, 2)
)`} punchcard />

Each sound has its own euclidean pattern. They interlock to create complex polyrhythms.

## Euclidean with Melodies

Euclidean patterns work with any sound, including melodic ones:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("arpy").euclid(3, 8).note("c3 e3 g3")`} punchcard />

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`note("c2 e2 g2 b2").s("bass").euclid(5, 16)`} punchcard />

## Comparing Euclidean to Mini-Notation

You can also write euclidean rhythms using mini-notation syntax `(beats, steps, offset)`:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd(3,8)")`} punchcard />

This is equivalent to:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid(3, 8)`} punchcard />

The mini-notation version is more compact, but the function version is easier to modify with patterns.

## Dynamic Euclidean Rhythms

With the function version, you can pattern the parameters:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("bd").euclid("<3 5 7>", 8)`} punchcard />

The number of beats cycles through 3, 5, and 7 on each repetition.

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`sound("hh").euclid(5, "<8 16>", "<0 2 4>")`} punchcard />

## Challenge: Build a Beat

Create a four-part rhythm using euclidean patterns. Fill in the numbers:

<MiniRepl client:idle tune={`stack(
  sound("bd").euclid(3, 8),
  sound("sd").euclid(2, 8, 4),
  sound("hh").euclid(7, 8),
  sound("perc").euclid(3, 16)
)`} punchcard />

**Hints:**
- Kick: `3, 8` or `4, 16` for a solid foundation
- Snare: `2, 8` with rotation for backbeat
- Hi-hat: denser works well—`5, 8` or `7, 8`
- Perc: odd numbers create interest—`3, 16` or `5, 12`

## What You Learned

- `euclid(beats, steps)` distributes beats evenly
- Third parameter rotates the pattern
- Classic rhythms (tresillo, clave, bell pattern) are euclidean
- Layering creates polyrhythms
- Parameters can be patterned for variation
